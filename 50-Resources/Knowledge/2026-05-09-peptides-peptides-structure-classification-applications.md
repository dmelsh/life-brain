# Peptides: Structure, Classification, and Applications

## Summary
Peptides are short chains of amino acids linked by peptide bonds, playing crucial roles in biological systems. They are classified by their source and function, ranging from hormones and signaling molecules to antibiotics and components of venoms. Peptides are increasingly relevant in medicine and biotechnology, with significant applications in pharmaceuticals and diagnostics.

## Key Points
- Peptides are amino acid chains linked by peptide bonds; polypeptides are longer chains, and proteins are polypeptides over 10,000 Da.
- Peptides are broadly classified by their sources and functions, including plant, bacterial, venom, and various physiological peptides.
- Ribosomal peptides, often modified post-translationally, function as hormones and signaling molecules, while nonribosomal peptides are enzyme-assembled and common in microbes, plants, and fungi.
- Peptides engage in numerous protein interactions important for cell signaling and immune modulation, making them a significant focus in pharmaceutical development.
- Machine learning is extensively used to classify, screen, and design peptides, overcoming the limitations of experimental screening in various functional classes such as antimicrobial and anticancer peptides.

## Notable
> Studies have reported that 15-40% of all protein–protein interactions in human cells are mediated by peptides.
